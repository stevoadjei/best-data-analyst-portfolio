A Comprehensive Analysis of Minimum Wage Policy Using the Modern Wage Dynamics Model

             
                Focus: Germany                 Simulation Modeling                 View Full Paper (PDF)              
           
                       
             

Project Overview & Research Voids

             

This paper analyzes the introduction of **Germany's statutory minimum wage** (effective January 1, 2015), amidst growing concerns over income inequality. The study specifically investigates the effects on **disposable household income**, **employment**, and **labor market inequality**.

             

We use the **Modern Wage Dynamics (MWD) simulation model** applied to Germany's unique context, which features a strong welfare state, social insurance, and high marginal effective taxes on low earnings. The model accurately represents the intricate interplay between the labor market and the larger economy.

                           

Core Research Questions:

             
                   
  • How does an increase in minimum wage affect **overall employment levels**, particularly among low-wage workers?
    •                
  • How does a rise in minimum wage influence the **income distribution** among different workforces?
    •                
  • What are the effects of a higher minimum wage on the overall **economic growth and productivity levels**?
    •

Conceptual Modeling (Modern Wage Dynamics)

             

The **MWD model (Version 1.0.0, 2022)**, originally conceptualized by J. M. Applegate, is an advanced agent-based model used to simulate wage determination in modern economic systems. It captures the complexity of interactions between wage rates, employment, and production, enabling the elaboration of relationships between **labor and goods markets**.

Simulation Experiments & Key Results

                           

Experiment 1: Employment (H_W)

                           
                   
  • **Finding:** A moderate minimum wage raise led to a **slight decline in total weekly hours worked (H_W)**.
    •                
  • **Implication:** Employers are likely to cut down on working hours to offset increased wage costs. However, large wage increases demonstrated a less pronounced reduction than expected, suggesting the damage to employment may be less severe than opponents fear.
    •              
             

Experiment 2: Income Distribution (Income)

                           
                   
  • **Finding:** Higher minimum wages had a **positive effect on the income level for the lower group** and reduced income inequality.
    •                
  • **Implication:** This was attested to by increased earnings in the 20th and 40th percentiles but minimal or no gains at the upper groups, making minimum wage a **strong policy tool for redistribution**.
    •              
             

Experiment 3: Economic Growth & Productivity (S_S and N)

                           
                   
  • **Finding:** Productivity (**N**) clearly **rose with a moderate minimum wage increase**, showing workers were arguably happier and more efficient.
    •                
  • **Trade-off:** In instances of high wage increases, productivity gains were offset by increased employer costs, leading to **flat or declining total output (S_S)** (economic growth).
    •              
           
                       
             

Conclusion & Policy Recommendations

             

Overall Policy Insight:

             

The study found that **moderate minimum wage increases** represent a balanced approach, conferring benefits on workers (inequality reduction, higher productivity) while minimizing negative effects on employment and output.

                           

Key Recommendations:

             
                   
  • Policymakers should implement **gradual minimum wage increases** rather than sudden ones, as incremental adjustments help firms adapt.
    •                
  • A uniform national minimum wage may not be effective due to regional and industrial diversity; a **regionally tailored approach** is essential.
    •                
  • **Continuous monitoring and assessment** are vital to facilitate timely interventions and adjustments in keeping with economic feedback.
    •
